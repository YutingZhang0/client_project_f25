Resume Support

Craft a Resume that Tells Your Story

Your resume is often your first impression—an opportunity to highlight your strengths, skills, and impact. Whether you’re starting from scratch or polishing an existing draft, the guidance and resources here are tailored to UMSI students and aligned with employer expectations.

Need feedback? You can always meet with a CDO Career Coach to review your materials.

Before You Start: Quick Checklist

  • Clarify your target role(s) so your content aligns with the job description.
  • Gather relevant experiences: internships, projects, courses, volunteering, part-time roles.
  • List concrete outcomes you achieved (metrics, scope, time/quality improvements).
  • Decide a clear structure: header, education, experience, projects, skills (plus optional sections).

Structure & Templates

Recruiters scan quickly. Use a clean layout with consistent headings and spacing. Most early-career resumes are one page; experienced candidates may use two pages.

  • UMSI Resume Guide and Rubric — formatting rules, examples, and expectations.
  • Resume Rubric — evaluate your resume with the same criteria used by the CDO.

Write Impactful Bullets with STAR

Strong bullets focus on outcomes, not just tasks. Use the STAR pattern (Situation, Task, Action, Result) and quantify results where possible.

  • Weak: “Worked on data dashboard.”
  • Better: “Built a data dashboard in Tableau to visualize weekly KPIs.”
  • Best: “Built a Tableau dashboard to visualize weekly KPIs for 5 product teams, reducing manual reporting time by 30%.”

Practice by drafting multiple versions and then tightening the language.

Tailor for Each Role (Keywords & ATS)

  1. Extract 5-8 essential keywords from the target job description (skills, tools, methods).
  2. Mirror those terms in your bullets where accurate—do not fabricate experience.
  3. Prioritize the most relevant experiences and place them higher on the page.
  4. Use a simple layout and standard section headings so Applicant Tracking Systems can parse your content.

Use Job Descriptions to Strengthen Your Resume

Treat the job description like an answer key: check whether your bullets reflect the responsibilities and must-have skills.

  • Highlight high-frequency verbs and tools (e.g., research, prototype, SQL, Python) and reflect them authentically.
  • Integrate keywords naturally into outcome-focused bullets.
  • Avoid long “technology inventories”; show how the tools produced results.

How to Add Course Projects

Course projects are valuable—especially when they include real data, users, or stakeholders.

  • Context: Purpose and audience (who benefited).
  • Approach: Methods, tools, dataset/source, your specific responsibilities.
  • Outcome: Quantified or validated results (accuracy, adoption, time savings).
  • Optional: If public, link to a repo, report, or demo.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Dense paragraphs—prefer concise, scannable bullets.
  • Listing responsibilities without outcomes—add metrics, scope, or impact.
  • Inconsistent formatting (dates, dashes, spacing, capitalization).
  • Long technology lists without context—explain how you used them.
  • Typos—proofread and ask a friend/coach to review.
